The term "rebound" has been frequently appearing in weight loss circles lately, medically known as "weight regain." Weight loss is a common concern for almost everyone in modern society. Some diet, some exercise intensely, and others follow various popular " intermittent fasting ," " ketogenic diets , " or " meal replacements ." In the short term, weight does indeed drop rapidly, but after a year, many find that the weight has returned, sometimes even more than before. This is the so-called " yo-yo effect ," where weight fluctuates like a yo-yo, eventually returning to its starting point.

So why do some people always fail to lose weight? The reason is actually quite simple: many people treat weight loss as a sprint, not a marathon! Rapid weight loss often leads to rapid rebound. The real key is long-term lifestyle changes. Of course, the emergence of GLP-1 drugs such as semaglutide and telpolide has also provided new possibilities for weight loss.

We keep talking about a rebound, but what exactly is a rebound, and what are the standards? Many people can't explain it clearly.

A weight regain of more than 3%–5% of the weight loss is considered a rebound. For example, if you lost 10 kilograms but later gained back 3–5 kilograms, it is considered a rebound. This is a commonly used metric in clinical research and nutrition.

If your weight returns to or even exceeds your pre-diet weight, this is a serious rebound!

So, why did this rebound occur?

From a physiological perspective, when you reduce your calorie intake during weight loss, your body automatically enters an "energy-saving mode." At this time, your basal metabolic rate decreases, much like your phone enters a power-saving mode. However, simultaneously, your ghrelin levels rise, making you feel hungry more easily, while your leptin levels drop, making it even harder to feel full. The result is that although you are indeed eating very little, your body will frantically store energy, leading to weight rebound.


Secondly, the psychological pressure is immense for people trying to lose weight, as the time constraint creates significant stress. Once they relax, they are very prone to "compensatory eating," or even binge eating. Binge eating after dieting is often more severe than normal eating, leading to a rapid weight regain.

Another issue is that many people set very strict diet and exercise plans during their weight loss period, including three low-to-moderate intensity training sessions a week, 7,000 brisk steps a day, and following the monthly diet plan I provided. However, once they reach their goals, they slack off! They fail to maintain these good eating habits and start staying up late again! The lack of long-term good habits is the biggest behavioral factor contributing to the rebound.

Finally, from an environmental perspective, there are too many drinking buddies! This is somewhat similar to smoking; everyone has some drinking buddies around them. I don't mean to be derogatory, but it's really important to learn to say no. Chinese traditions are numerous, with frequent family gatherings, unavoidable holiday meals, and of course, a culture of social obligations. All of these present a huge challenge for those trying to lose weight. Learning to say no to "delicious food and fine wine" is a major test!


After all this rambling, how do I prevent a rebound?

I believe the correct approach to weight loss should be: scientific, long-term, and habitual.

First and foremost, you must control your total energy intake. Make it a habit to check the calorie count of the food you buy and learn some basic nutrition knowledge. But don't go on extreme diets. I always emphasize that you should eat three meals a day regularly and on time. You can eat less, but you can't skip meals. Weight loss is not about starving yourself!

In addition, it's essential to ensure sufficient intake of high-quality protein daily, ideally boiled eggs. Those who exercise can supplement with protein powder, as weight loss requires energy! You can't lose weight without energy, so it's also important to maintain muscle mass.

Another important aspect is dietary fiber. Eating more of it can improve the gut environment. At the same time, it's crucial to avoid a single-food diet. Some people believe that eating only fruit will help them lose weight, but grapes, lychees, and bananas are all high in sugar and calories. As for fruit juice, which I mentioned a few days ago, not only does it lack fiber, but the sugar is also absorbed by the body more quickly. These are all misconceptions about weight loss, and meal replacements are even more so.

In short: Eat less, but eat right.

In addition, even if you are using weight loss medication, you still need to exercise, but the intensity should be moderate.

Depending on your individual circumstances, you can do some aerobic exercises, such as brisk walking or swimming. If you go to the gym, you can do some strength training such as squats, push-ups, or dumbbell exercises. For daily commutes, you can climb stairs or walk to work, depending on your individual situation. The key is to get moving. The intensity must be moderate or low-to-moderate; some people even need low intensity. There's a common misconception that many people train at high intensity but don't lose weight . This is because they burn too many calories during workouts, while also overeating, even exceeding their calorie expenditure. Furthermore, prolonged high-intensity training without sufficient rest leads to a significant increase in the stress hormone cortisol . All of these factors make weight loss very difficult.
